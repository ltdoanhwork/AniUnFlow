Early optimization emphasizes stable object correspondence and conservative motion priors; only later do we activate stronger dense and temporal regularization. \section{Training Dynamics and Optimization Behavior}

It is also useful to show how AniUnFlow was actually trained, rather than only presenting final checkpoints and qualitative panels. The optimization process already reflects the main design philosophy of the framework: the model is not trained as a single monolithic dense matcher, but as a staged objective in which structure, propagation memory, dense recovery, and boundary correction are gradually activated.

At a high level, the training objective can be written as
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{AniUnFlow}} =
\mathcal{L}_{\text{base}}
 + \lambda_{\text{struct}} \mathcal{L}_{\text{struct}}
 + \lambda_{\text{memory}} \mathcal{L}_{\text{memory}}
 + \lambda_{\text{dense}} \mathcal{L}_{\text{dense}}
 + \lambda_{\text{res}} \mathcal{L}_{\text{res}}
 + \lambda_{\text{long}} \mathcal{L}_{\text{long}},
\label{eq:training_objective_grouped}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}_{\text{base}}$ contains the standard photometric, smoothness, and short-term consistency terms, while the remaining groups collect the segmentation warp, SAM-memory consistency, dense-slot/global refinement, residual correction, and long-gap propagation losses. In practice, these terms are not all emphasized from the first epoch. Instead, the training schedule follows four phases: an initial warm-up stage (epochs 1--8), a residual-enabled stage (epochs 9--16), a larger-stride stage (epochs 17--32), and finally a long-gap propagation stage beginning at epoch 33.

Even with provisional values, the logic of the loss decomposition is already clear from the design of the framework. The structural term $\mathcal{L}_{\text{struct}}$ anchors the motion field to segment-aware organization, which is especially important in animation frames with large flat-color regions and weak local texture. The memory term $\mathcal{L}_{\text{memory}}$ promotes temporal consistency across those motion units, reducing the chance that the model will treat each frame pair as an isolated matching problem. The dense term $\mathcal{L}_{\text{dense}}$ then restores local articulated detail that the object prior alone cannot represent well, while the residual term $\mathcal{L}_{\text{res}}$ specializes on the narrow contour-sensitive regions where even the refined dense estimate remains insufficient.

From an ablation perspective, the most important expectation is not that every loss term affects every metric equally, but that different terms target different failure modes. Removing structure- or memory-oriented supervision should primarily hurt region coherence and temporal support. Removing dense refinement should most clearly reduce local geometric accuracy. Removing the residual objective should have its strongest effect on boundaries, line alignment, and thin deforming parts. Finally, removing the long-gap term should mostly reduce robustness once the temporal stride becomes more challenging, even if short-range behavior remains comparatively stable.

This distinction matters for the thesis because it clarifies how AniUnFlow should be evaluated. A useful loss-term ablation is not only a ranking of scalar drops; it is a map from objective terms to animation-specific behaviors. Even in this provisional form, Table~\ref{tab:loss_ablation_template} communicates that the strongest degradation is expected when structural organization is removed, while memory, dense refinement, and boundary correction each contribute in a more targeted manner. In the finalized version, this table should ideally be paired with one or two qualitative examples showing the characteristic failure mode that becomes visible when each major term is removed.

Table~\ref{tab:branch_progression} should be interpreted carefully. It is not meant to claim that every later branch dominates every earlier branch on every accessible checkpoint. Instead, it helps explain the research direction of AniUnFlow. The dense object-memory branch improves threshold accuracy by letting local matching restore articulated detail that the slot prior alone cannot explain. In other words, the later AniUnFlow design is not simply ``more modules''; it is a stronger attempt to make structure and dense correspondence cooperate rather than compete.

\section{Interpretability and XAI Perspective}

It is reasonable to ask whether AniUnFlow can be discussed from an explainable-AI perspective. The short answer is yes, but the explanation protocol must be adapted to dense correspondence rather than copied from classification-style saliency analysis. In optical flow, the central question is not simply where the network ``looked,'' but which structural cues supported the predicted motion, where the model became uncertain, and where corrective updates were required. For this reason, the most useful XAI view for AniUnFlow is a stage-aware visual protocol built around interpretable intermediate maps.

These signals explain where the model receives strong structural support and where temporal structure begins to diverge.}

Figure~\ref{fig:xai_structure} focuses on the structure-first side of the framework. The SAM labels reveal the motion units used by the model, while the boundary overlay makes the contour-sensitive regions explicit. The disagreement map is especially useful because it surfaces a failure precursor rather than only a failure consequence: when propagated structure and the current segmentation no longer agree, the model should become cautious even before the final endpoint error becomes obvious. This is a more meaningful XAI signal for animation than a generic attention heatmap because it is directly tied to region identity, contour stability, and temporal consistency.

Figure~\ref{fig:xai_diagnostics} complements the structural view with a repair-oriented view. Residual magnitude shows where the boundary-aware correction branch is forced to intervene strongly, which typically corresponds to thin contours, articulated edges, and other regions where the coarse structural prior is insufficient by itself. The EPE heatmap then acts as a sanity-check signal: it tells the reader whether these difficult regions are actually where the final prediction remains challenging. Read together, the two figures provide a compact but useful explanation protocol: \emph{structure} explains where the motion estimate is supported, \emph{disagreement} explains where temporal support weakens, and \emph{residual/error} explains where the model still needs active correction.

This reduced XAI presentation is deliberate. In principle, AniUnFlow exposes many internal states, but not every raw tensor is equally suitable for publication. For the paper, it is more valuable to keep only the signals that remain visually stable, semantically interpretable, and clearly related to animation-specific failure modes. Under that standard, structural partitions, boundary cues, disagreement maps, residual activity, and localized error are the most convincing explanatory artifacts. They are also the signals that were most useful during model development, since they make it easier to distinguish structural mismatch from local contour-repair behavior.

At the same time, the current thesis should not overclaim. AniUnFlow is interpretable in a strong practical sense, but it is not yet a complete causal-XAI system. A fuller explainability study would include explicit counterfactual experiments such as perturbing SAM masks, suppressing propagation-memory guidance, or tracing how individual object slots alter the final flow in selected regions. Even so, the present framework already provides a much stronger basis for explanation than a purely opaque dense matcher, and that makes it a promising starting point for explanation-oriented animation tools in the future.

\section{Additional Downstream Propagation Tasks}

The same logic extends beyond frame interpolation. If the predicted flow is genuinely useful for animation, it should also support other propagation-style operations that artists and downstream systems care about. To test this, we evaluate four additional flow-driven tasks on the same difficult pair: mask propagation, color propagation, contour propagation, and temporally consistent stylization transfer.

\paragraph{Experimental setup.}
We select the challenging pair \texttt{0306 $\rightarrow$ 0307} from \texttt{cami\_02\_05\_A}, because it contains noticeable head motion, thin mouth structure, and narrow background elements. The comparison is always between the object-memory baseline and AniUnFlow, using exactly the same source frame and target frame. No task-specific fine-tuning or extra propagation network is added; the only difference is the estimated flow field.

Let $I_t$ and $I_{t+1}$ denote the source and target frames, and let $\mathbf{F}_{t \rightarrow t+1}$ be the predicted forward flow. For a generic source signal $S_t$ defined on frame $t$, propagation is implemented by backward warping:
\begin{equation}
\hat{S}_{t+1}(\mathbf{x}) = S_t\bigl(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{F}_{t \rightarrow t+1}(\mathbf{x})\bigr),
\label{eq:generic_propagation}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{x}$ is a pixel location in the target frame. In practice this is implemented with bilinear sampling for dense signals such as RGB appearance or stylized images. For discrete masks, we first convert the source labels into a one-hot tensor and then warp each channel before taking the maximum-response label:
\begin{equation}
\hat{M}_{t+1}(\mathbf{x}) = \arg\max_{k} \; \mathcal{W}\!\left(M_t^{(k)}, \mathbf{F}_{t \rightarrow t+1}\right)(\mathbf{x}),
\label{eq:mask_propagation}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{W}$ denotes the same warping operator and $k$ indexes the label channels. This setup isolates the practical effect of flow quality: if the motion field is structurally sound, the propagated signal should remain aligned with the target drawing; otherwise the result will show drift, ghosting, or doubled edges.

For color propagation, we simply set $S_t = I_t$ in Eq.~\ref{eq:generic_propagation}. This produces a direct test of whether the predicted flow is useful for carrying painted appearance from one drawing to the next. Figure~\ref{fig:color_propagation} shows that the baseline introduces heavier ghosting around the eye, teeth, and jawline, whereas AniUnFlow produces a visibly cleaner warped image. This matters for anime-oriented applications such as color carry-over, paint assistance, and temporally coherent editing, where even a small geometric mistake becomes obvious once the image itself is warped.

This is strong evidence that the benefit of AniUnFlow is not merely lower scalar error, but better preservation of line-based structure that actually matters in hand-drawn animation.

\subsection{Stylization Consistency}

\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{img/downstream_tasks/cami_02_05_A_0306_stylization_consistency.png}

Finally, we test a simple stylization-consistency setting. We first construct a stylized source rendering $T(I_t)$ through a lightweight posterization-and-edge pipeline, and then propagate it with the estimated flow:
\begin{equation}
\hat{I}^{\text{style}}_{t+1}(\mathbf{x}) = T(I_t)\bigl(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{F}_{t \rightarrow t+1}(\mathbf{x})\bigr).
\label{eq:stylization_propagation}
\end{equation}
The purpose is not to benchmark stylization quality itself, but to test whether the flow is stable enough to support temporal effect transfer. In Figure~\ref{fig:stylization_consistency}, AniUnFlow preserves the stylized line arrangement and silhouette more faithfully, while the baseline output contains more obvious instability around the head and facial edges. This suggests that AniUnFlow is better suited to downstream pipelines where flow is used to stabilize frame-to-frame appearance transformations.

Taken together, Figures~\ref{fig:mask_propagation}--\ref{fig:stylization_consistency} show that AniUnFlow is useful not only as a flow estimator, but also as a reusable motion prior for practical animation operations. In production-oriented tools, flow is rarely the final product. It is more often an internal mechanism that drives mask transfer, color carry-over, contour stabilization, and temporally consistent effects. The downstream evaluation therefore strengthens the main argument of this thesis: a structure-aware motion field is valuable precisely because it remains reliable when reused by other tasks.
